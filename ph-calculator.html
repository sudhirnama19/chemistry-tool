Understanding pH in Chemistry

The pH of a solution measures its acidity or basicity. It is defined as the negative logarithm of hydrogen ion concentration.

Formula: pH = −log[H⁺]

Where [H⁺] represents hydrogen ion concentration in mol/L.

Examples of pH Values

  • Battery Acid → pH ≈ 1
  • Lemon Juice → pH ≈ 2
  • Vinegar → pH ≈ 3
  • Pure Water → pH = 7
  • Soap Solution → pH ≈ 10
  • Ammonia → pH ≈ 11

Explanation of pH

The pH scale is used to measure the acidity or basicity of a chemical solution. It is defined as the negative logarithm of the hydrogen ion concentration.

Formula: pH = −log[H⁺]

If the hydrogen ion concentration increases, the pH decreases and the solution becomes more acidic. If hydrogen ion concentration decreases, the solution becomes more basic.

Example 1: Strong Acid

If the hydrogen ion concentration is:

[H⁺] = 10⁻³ M

pH = −log(10⁻³)
pH = 3

This indicates a strongly acidic solution.

Example 2: Neutral Solution

Pure water at 25°C has:

[H⁺] = 10⁻⁷ M

pH = 7

This is considered a neutral solution.

Example 3: Basic Solution

If the hydroxide concentration is:

[OH⁻] = 10⁻² M

pOH = 2 pH = 14 − 2 = 12

This represents a basic solution.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the pH scale?

The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14 and measures how acidic or basic a solution is. Values below 7 are acidic, 7 is neutral, and values above 7 are basic.

Why is pH important in chemistry?

pH helps chemists understand the chemical behavior of acids and bases. It is important in chemical reactions, biological systems, environmental science, and industrial processes.
