The pH Concept

pH measures the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution. It is mathematically defined as:

$$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+]$$

For bases, we calculate $pOH$ where $\text{pH} + \text{pOH} = 14$.

Understanding pH & Calculations

What is pH?

pH is a logarithmic scale used to specify the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution. It is defined as the negative base-10 logarithm of the molar concentration of hydrogen ions ($H^+$).

Standard Formula: $$pH = -\log_{10}[H^+]$$ $$pOH = -\log_{10}[OH^-]$$ $$pH + pOH = 14 \text{ (at 25°C)}$$
Calculation Example

Problem: Find the pH of a $0.001\text{ M}$ solution of $HCl$.

  • $HCl$ is a strong acid, so $[H^+] = 0.001\text{ M}$ (or $10^{-3}\text{ M}$).
  • $pH = -\log(10^{-3})$
  • Result: $pH = 3.0$

Research & Measurement Techniques

In professional laboratories and research (like GATE/CSIR labs), pH is measured using more than just paper strips:

  • Potentiometric Method: Using a glass electrode and a pH meter to measure the voltage difference between a reference and a sensing electrode.
  • Colorimetric Analysis: Utilizing spectrophotometry with pH-sensitive dyes to measure absorbance at specific wavelengths.
  • Micro-pH Sensors: Used in biological research to measure the pH within a single cell or small tissue samples.
  • Conductometric Titration: Monitoring the change in electrical conductivity to determine the equivalence point of acid-base reactions.

Unique Acid & Base Facts

The strongest acid: Fluoroantimonic acid ($HSbF_6$) is a "superacid" that is $2 \times 10^{19}$ times stronger than $100\%$ sulfuric acid.

The universal solvent: Pure water is amphoteric, meaning it can act as both an acid and a base through self-ionization.

Biological pH: Human blood must maintain a very strict pH range of $7.35$ to $7.45$. Even a small shift can be fatal.

Expert FAQ: pH & Acid-Base Equilibrium

Can pH be negative?

Yes. If the $[H^+]$ concentration is greater than $1\text{ M}$ (e.g., concentrated $HCl$), the log calculation results in a negative value.

Does temperature affect pH?

Absolutely. As temperature increases, the auto-ionization of water increases, lowering the neutral point below $7.0$.

What is a Buffer Solution?

A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added, usually made of a weak acid and its conjugate base.

Why is pH 7 neutral?

At $25^\circ C$, the concentration of $[H^+]$ and $[OH^-]$ in pure water are equal at $10^{-7}\text{ M}$.

What is the "Common Ion Effect"?

The suppression of the ionization of a weak electrolyte by adding a strong electrolyte that shares a common ion.

Difference between Strong and Weak Acids?

Strong acids ionize $100\%$ in water, while weak acids only partially ionize, establishing an equilibrium.

How is pH related to $pK_a$?

When $pH = pK_a$, the concentrations of the acid and its conjugate base are exactly equal (Henderson-Hasselbalch equation).

Why is the ocean's pH falling?

Increased $CO_2$ absorption from the atmosphere forms carbonic acid, leading to ocean acidification.

What is $K_w$?

It is the ionic product of water, $1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ at $25^\circ C$.

What is the pH of the human stomach?

The stomach contains $HCl$ with a pH of roughly $1.5$ to $3.5$, necessary for protein digestion.
