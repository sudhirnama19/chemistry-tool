Acid-Base Titration Theory

Titration is a quantitative analytical technique used to determine the unknown concentration of an analyte by reacting it with a standard solution (titrant) of known concentration.

Key Equations:
  • Equivalence Point: $N_1V_1 = N_2V_2$ (or $n_1M_1V_1 = n_2M_2V_2$)
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch (Buffer Region): $pH = pK_a + \log \frac{[\text{Salt}]}{[\text{Acid}]}$
  • At Half-Equivalence: $pH = pK_a$
Example: Titrating $50\text{ mL}$ of $0.1\text{ M } HCl$ with $0.1\text{ M } NaOH$.
The equivalence point occurs at exactly $50\text{ mL}$ of $NaOH$ added, where the $pH$ jumps from roughly $3$ to $11$ in a single drop.

Expert FAQ & Concepts

1. Why does the pH change so rapidly near the equivalence point?

Near the equivalence point, the analyte is almost exhausted. Even a fractional drop of titrant causes a massive logarithmic shift in $[H^+]$ concentration.

2. What is the difference between End Point and Equivalence Point?

The Equivalence Point is the theoretical stoichiometric completion. The End Point is where the indicator actually changes color.

3. Which indicator is best for Weak Acid vs Strong Base?

Phenolphthalein is ideal because its color change interval (pH 8.2–10) matches the basic pH found at the equivalence point of weak acids.

4. Why does a diprotic acid show two "steps"?

It has two separate protons with different $K_a$ values. The base neutralizes the first $H^+$ completely before moving to the second, provided $K_{a1} \gg K_{a2}$.

5. What is the "Half-Equivalence" point?

This occurs when volume added $V = V_{eq}/2$. Here, $[Acid] = [Salt]$, so $pH = pK_a$. This is the point of maximum buffering capacity.

6. Can very weak acids ($K_a < 10^{-7}$) be titrated?

Standard indicators fail because the pH jump is too small. Conductometric titration or pH meters (like this simulator) are required.

7. How does concentration affect the titration curve?

Higher concentrations result in a longer vertical "inflection" region, making the end point much easier to detect accurately.

8. What is Back Titration?

Used for slow reactions or insoluble solids. An excess reagent is added, and the remaining unreacted portion is titrated.
