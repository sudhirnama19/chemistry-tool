What is a Limiting Reagent?

The Limiting Reagent (or limiting reactant) is the substance that is totally consumed when the chemical reaction is complete. The amount of product formed is limited by this reagent, since the reaction cannot continue without it.

Core Formulas:
1. Moles (n) = Mass (m) / Molar Mass (M)
2. Comparison Ratio = Moles / Stoichiometric Coefficient
3. Theoretical Yield = Moles of LR × (Product Coef / LR Coef) × Product Molar Mass

Rules of Limiting Reagents

Rule Application
Law of Conservation Mass of reactants must equal mass of products.
The Ratio Rule Lowest mole-to-coefficient ratio is ALWAYS the Limiting Reagent.
Excess Reagent Substances left over after the LR is consumed.

Scientific Research & Applications

In industrial chemical synthesis and pharmaceutical research, determining the limiting reagent is critical for Atom Economy. Researchers use these calculations to:

  • Minimize expensive catalyst waste.
  • Calculate Percent Yield to optimize reaction conditions.
  • Manage safety in exothermic reactions by controlling the LR feed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a product be a limiting reagent?

No, limiting reagents are always reactants.

What if ratios are equal?

Both reactants are consumed simultaneously; there is no LR.

Why is actual yield lower than theoretical?

Due to side reactions, impurities, or incomplete recovery.

How do I handle scientific notation?

Our calculator supports 'e' notation, e.g., 6.022e23.
