Comprehensive Guide to Chemical Kinetics

Understanding Reaction Rate

The Reaction Rate is the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time. It is influenced by the nature of reactants, concentration, surface area, and most critically, Temperature.

Rate $= -\frac{d[A]}{dt} = k[A]^n$

Arrhenius Theory & Activation Energy ($E_a$)

Svante Arrhenius proposed that for a reaction to occur, particles must possess a minimum energy called Activation Energy ($E_a$). If the energy of collision is less than $E_a$, the particles simply bounce off each other without reacting.

$k = A \cdot e^{-\frac{E_a}{RT}}$

When comparing two temperatures, we use the integrated form:

$\ln\left(\frac{k_2}{k_1}\right) = \frac{E_a}{R} \left( \frac{T_2 - T_1}{T_1 T_2} \right)$

Collision Theory: The Physics of Reaction

Collision theory provides the quantitative basis for the pre-exponential factor ($A$). It states that the rate depends on:

  • Collision Frequency ($Z$): The number of collisions per second.
  • Orientation Factor ($P$): The probability that particles collide at the correct angle.
  • Energy Factor: The fraction of molecules having energy $\ge E_a$.

Half-Life ($t_{1/2}$) and its Significance

The Half-Life of a reaction is the time required for the reactant concentration to decrease to half of its initial value. It is a vital tool for determining the order of a reaction.

Zero Order: $t_{1/2} = \frac{[A]_0}{2k}$
First Order: $t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{k}$

Frequently Asked Questions

How does a catalyst affect Activation Energy?

A catalyst provides an alternative mechanism with a lower activation energy, allowing a greater fraction of molecules to have sufficient energy to react at the same temperature.

Why is the 1st Order Half-Life independent of concentration?

In first-order kinetics, the rate is proportional to concentration. As concentration drops, the rate drops proportionally, keeping the time to reach the 50% mark constant.
