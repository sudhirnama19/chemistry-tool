Normality ($N$) & Equivalents

Normality describes the concentration of reactive species (equivalents) per liter of solution. It is the primary unit for titration analysis.

$N = \frac{\text{Mass (g)}}{\text{Eq. Wt} \times \text{Volume (L)}}$
$\text{Equivalent Weight} = \frac{\text{Molar Mass}}{\text{n-factor}}$

JEE Advanced & GATE Insights

Normality is crucial in Redox Titrations. The law of equivalence states that at the end point, equivalents of oxidizing agent = equivalents of reducing agent ($N_1V_1 = N_2V_2$).

CSIR-NET Difficulty: Advanced problems involve "Double Titration" (e.g., $Na_2CO_3$ and $NaHCO_3$ mixture). Always calculate the n-factor based on the specific indicator used (Phenolphthalein vs Methyl Orange).

Scientific Research & FAQs
Q: Is Normality temperature-dependent?
A: Yes. Because volume changes with temperature, Normality is not thermally stable. In high-precision research, Molality is often used instead.
Unique Fact: For mono-protic acids like $HCl$, Molarity and Normality are identical ($n=1$).
