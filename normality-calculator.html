Normality ($N$) Definition

Normality measures the molar concentration of reactive equivalents in a solution. It is essential for titrations where reactions are 1:1 in terms of equivalents.

$N = \frac{\text{Gram Equivalents of Solute}}{\text{Volume of Solution (L)}}$
$\text{Gram Eq} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Equivalent Weight}}$

Competitive Exam Insights

JEE & GATE Level: In redox titrations, $n$-factor is the total number of electrons lost or gained per molecule. For example, in acidic $KMnO_4$, $n=5$.

Research Note: Normality is volume-dependent, meaning it changes with temperature. For high-precision thermal research (CSIR-NET), Molality is often preferred as it depends on mass.

Common FAQs
Q: Why is $N \ge M$?
A: Since $N = M \times \text{n-factor}$ and n-factor is always $\ge 1$, Normality can never be smaller than Molarity.

Q: Units of Normality?
A: It is expressed as equivalents per liter ($eq/L$) or simply '$N$'.
