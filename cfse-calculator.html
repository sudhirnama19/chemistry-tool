Crystal Field Stabilization Energy (CFSE)

CFSE is the stability gained by a transition metal complex through the splitting of degenerate $d$-orbitals into sets of different energies ($t_{2g}$ and $e_g$) in the presence of a ligand field.

$\text{CFSE}_{oct} = [-0.4n(t_{2g}) + 0.6n(e_g)]\Delta_o + nP$
$\text{CFSE}_{tet} = [-0.6n(e) + 0.4n(t_2)]\Delta_t$

Where $n$ is the number of electrons, $\Delta$ is the splitting parameter, and $P$ is the pairing energy.

Interpretation & Splitting Rules

Octahedral Splitting: The $d_{z^2}$ and $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbitals point directly at ligands, rising in energy ($e_g$), while others drop ($t_{2g}$).
Tetrahedral Splitting: The reverse occurs. However, because ligands do not point directly at any orbital, $\Delta_t$ is always smaller ($\approx 4/9 \Delta_o$).

Scientific Applications

Color Chemistry: The $\Delta$ value determines the wavelength of light absorbed. Research into OLEDs uses CFSE to tune the exact emission color of Iridium complexes.

Catalysis: CFSE explains the "Inertness" of $d^3$ and $low\text{-}spin\ d^6$ complexes (like $[Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}$), which is vital for designing stable industrial catalysts.
